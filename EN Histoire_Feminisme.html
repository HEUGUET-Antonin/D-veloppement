Music from 1848 to 1911

La marseillaise des cotillons – 1848

"La marseillaise des cotillons" is a feminist anthem based on the national anthem "La marseillaise". The text was written by Louise de Chaumont and appeared in the newspaper "La République des femmes, journal des cotillons" in 1848. The lyrics denounce the inequality man woman and make the apology of the female sex.

Fille d'ouvriers – 1898

"Fille d'ouvriers" is a song by Jules Jouy from 1898. The song describes the conditions The song describes the conditions of women workers of the time by tracing their history from birth to death. The text evokes their difficult work, their poverty and the crimes that these women suffer: rape and prostitution. This song has been performed many times until 2016 by Agnès Bihl.

Bread and Roses - 1911

"Bread and Roses" is a poem by James Oppenheim published in 1911 in "The American Magazine". James Oppenheim dedicated the poem "to the women of the West" and the title was used as a as a slogan for the "Bread and Roses strike". With this slogan, the demonstrators are demanding better pay and working conditions.

Music from 1911 to 1975

Hymne du MLF – 1971

The "MLF Anthem" is a song created collectively in 1971 by feminist activists. This song has become an emblem of the women's liberation movement and more generally of the French feminist struggles. The refrain of this music is taken up from time to time as a as a slogan during demonstrations: "Let's get up, slave women, and break our shackles. Up, up, up!

Run the World (Girls) – 2011

"Run the World" is a single from the singer Beyoncé which was released in 2011. The music highlights the empowerment of women, their emancipation. It highlights the strength, the power of women, indeed, in her chorus Beyoncé repeats the following verse about ten times "Who run the world? Girls!"

La grenade – 2017

"La grenade" is a music by Clara Luciani released in 2017. The song, which is very topical, has quickly become a real feminist anthem. In this song, Clara highlights the strength of women, particularly in the chorus, where the singer repeats the following line: "Under my breast, the pomegranate". She expresses here the fact that some people can underestimate a woman because of her She expresses the fact that some people may underestimate a woman because of her sex but that she can be dangerous: strong.
